Compute the Estimation of Value Efficiently[1]

In our simple stationary reinforcement learning task, like kkk-armed bandit that we have discussed in the post ‘An Introduction to kkk-armed Bandit Problem’ and ‘the 10-armed Testbed’. Under this strong assumption that each action has a stationary reward distribution, we use the estimation of reward to approximate the value of each action:

q⋆(a)≐E[Rt∣At=a](1)q_{\star}(a)\doteq \mathbb{E}[R_t|A_t=a]\tag{1} q⋆​(a)≐E[Rt​∣At​=a](1)

And we used the average rewards of an action aia_iai​:

Qn=R1+R2+⋯+Rn−1n−1(2)Q_{n} = \frac{R_1+R_2+\cdots+R_{n-1}}{n-1}\tag{2} Qn​=n−1R1​+R2​+⋯+Rn−1​​(2)

where QnQ_{n}Qn​ is the average of n−1n-1n−1 rewards, R1,R2,⋯ ,Rn−1R_1,R_2,\cdots,R_{n-1}R1​,R2​,⋯,Rn−1​, that the agent has recieved through action aia_iai​, to instead the estimation in equation (1).

However, to compute equation (2), we need to restore n−1n-1n−1 subtract and a division. Basing on the law of large numbers, we want as many steps as possible to get a more approximation of value by an average of rewards(‘the 10-armed Testbed’). So we need a very huge memory.

But if we consider the transformation of equation (2) this drawback would be no more:

Qn+1=R1+R2+⋯+Rnn=1n∑i=1nRi=1n(Rn+∑i=1n−1Ri)=1n(Rn+(n−1)1n−1∑i=1n−1Ri)=1n(Rn+(n−1)Qn)=1n(Rn−Qn+nQn)=Qn+1n(Rn−Qn)(3)\begin{aligned} Q_{n+1} &= \frac{R_1+R_2+\cdots+R_{n}}{n}\\ &=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^nR_i \\ &= \frac{1}{n}(R_n+\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}R_i)\\ &= \frac{1}{n}(R_n+(n-1)\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}R_i)\\ &= \frac{1}{n}(R_n+(n-1)Q_{n})\\ &= \frac{1}{n}(R_n-Q_{n}+nQ_{n})\\ &= Q_{n} + \frac{1}{n}(R_n-Q_{n}) \end{aligned}\tag{3} Qn+1​​=nR1​+R2​+⋯+Rn​​=n1​i=1∑n​Ri​=n1​(Rn​+i=1∑n−1​Ri​)=n1​(Rn​+(n−1)n−11​i=1∑n−1​Ri​)=n1​(Rn​+(n−1)Qn​)=n1​(Rn​−Qn​+nQn​)=Qn​+n1​(Rn​−Qn​)​(3)

if we use the equation (3) to calculate the average of rewards, we only need a multiplication, an addition and a subtract. This is far more efficient than equation (2).

The equation (3) is not only an efficient method for calculation but it represents some philosophy in reinforcement learning. In many reinforcement learning algorithms, they contain the update step:

NewEstimate←OldEstimate+stepsize×[Target−OldEstimate](4)\text{NewEstimate}\gets\text{OldEstimate}+\text{stepsize}\times[\text{Target}-\text{OldEstimate}]\tag{4} NewEstimate←OldEstimate+stepsize×[Target−OldEstimate](4)

This equation can be described as ‘take a step towards the target from old estimation’ in English. And the target of equation (3) is the nthn^{\text{th}}nth reward (or its estimation for precise). The difference, Target−OldEstimate\text{Target}-\text{OldEstimate}Target−OldEstimate, is called error in estimation . The step-size denoted as α\alphaα or αt(a)\alpha_t(a)αt​(a) in equation(3) is 1n\frac{1}{n}n1​ and it changes from step to step.
