Gradient Bandit Algorithms

We have discussed several methods to solve kkk-armed bandits in stationary or nonstationary environment.

  1. ‘An Introduction to kkk-armed Bandit Problem’
  2. ‘Incremental Implementation’
  3. ‘Optimistic Initial Value’
  4. ‘the 10-armed Testbed’

However, the action-value methods based on the philosophy that we select actions to achieve our finial goal – maximizing the total rewards in the whole process. The general idea is selecting action basing on some criterions.

So we introduce another method that we select actions basing on a numerical preference which represent how we like each action. If we love action aia_iai​ so much more than others, we select it. To measure how much we love each action, we define a function:

Ht(a)(1)H_t(a)\tag{1} Ht​(a)(1)

where ttt represents current time or step, aaa is a certain action. When Ht(ai)>Ht(aj)H_t(a_i)>H_t(a_j)Ht​(ai​)>Ht​(aj​) where i≠ji\neq ji​=j, we prefer action iii at time ttt. So the interval of the value of ‘numerical preference’ is not so important, but which one is relatively big is more attractive. In other words, the relation of Ht(a1)=0.01H_t(a_1)=0.01Ht​(a1​)=0.01 and Ht(a2)=0.001H_t(a_2)=0.001Ht​(a2​)=0.001 and the relation of Ht(a1)=100,001H_t(a_1)=100,001Ht​(a1​)=100,001 and Ht(a1)=100,000H_t(a_1)=100,000Ht​(a1​)=100,000 are equivalent. To make this strategy work, we first convert the numerical preference into some kind of probability distribution to sample action. The soft-max distribution

Pr{At=a}≐eHt(a)∑bkeHt(b)(2)\mathrm{Pr}\{A_t = a\}\doteq \frac{e^{H_t(a)}}{\sum_b^{k}e^{H_t(b)}}\tag{2} Pr{At​=a}≐∑bk​eHt​(b)eHt​(a)​(2)

where Ht(a)H_t(a)Ht​(a) is the numerical preference of action aaa that we select at step ttt, and the parameter bbb in denominator is a dummy variable. More details of softmax distribution can be found in the post ‘An Introduction to Probabilistic Generative Models’.

And the distribution defined in equation(2) represents the probabitly of taking action aaa at time ttt and we denote it as:

πt(a)≐eHt(a)∑bkeHt(b)(3)\pi_t(a)\doteq \frac{e^{H_t(a)}}{\sum_b^{k}e^{H_t(b)}}\tag{3} πt​(a)≐∑bk​eHt​(b)eHt​(a)​(3)

To the whole algorithm, we initial the preference of all actions to 000(i.e. H1(a)=0H_1(a)=0H1​(a)=0 for all aaa). And the idea of stochastic gradient ascent was employed to update the preference of each action by steps:

Ht+1(At)≐Ht(At)+α(Rt−Rˉt)(1−πt(At))(4)H_{t+1}(A_t)\doteq H_t(A_t) + \alpha (R_t-\bar{R}_t)(1-\pi_t(A_t))\tag{4} Ht+1​(At​)≐Ht​(At​)+α(Rt​−Rˉt​)(1−πt​(At​))(4)

and

Ht+1(a)≐Ht(a)−α(Rt−Rˉt)πt(At) for all a≠At(5)H_{t+1}(a)\doteq H_t(a) - \alpha (R_t-\bar{R}_t)\pi_t(A_t)\text{ for all }a\neq A_t\tag{5} Ht+1​(a)≐Ht​(a)−α(Rt​−Rˉt​)πt​(At​) for all a​=At​(5)

where RtR_tRt​ is the reward of current step ttt of selected action AtA_tAt​. α\alphaα is the step size. And Rˉt∈R\bar{R}_t\in \mathbb{R}Rˉt​∈R is the average of all rewards up through and including time ttt. This average can be calculated by ‘incremental implementation’ . Rˉt\bar{R}_tRˉt​ serves as a baseline, and if the selected action AtA_tAt​ got a reward greater than the baseline Rˉt\bar{R}_tRˉt​, the numerical preference of action AtA_tAt​ increases(equation(4)). And the preference of the other actions decrease(equation(5)). However, if the selected action AtA_tAt​ got a reward lower than the baseline Rˉt\bar{R}_tRˉt​, the numerical preference of action AtA_tAt​ decrease(equation(4)). And the preference of the other actions increase(equation(5)).
